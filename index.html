Advocates raise concerns over mayoral control of public schools as state lawmakers decide whether to keep Adams in charge

By Aissatou Diallo

Graphics by Lulu Wang and Kyara Torres-Olivares

December 22, 2023

Photo by Frances Cohen

The New York State Education Department will begin holding hearings this month as part of its “comprehensive review of mayoral control and assessment of the overall effectiveness of mayoral control of New York City schools.”

The review comes as many parents and teachers across the city have called for an end to mayoral control of the city’s public schools, which was set to expire in 2022 but was extended for two more years through a deal with state lawmakers.

Under the deal, the department mandated that Adams and New York City Department of Education Chancellor David Banks reduce class sizes by 2027, a policy that parents and teachers have advocated for for decades.

[Read more: NYC public schools set to begin reducing class sizes this September]

In anticipation of the 2022 agreement expiring in 2024, the New York State Education Department will hear oral testimony from all members of the public, including students, parents, teachers, school administration, and staff, as well as education policy and governance experts, over the next two months. Hearings are being held in person at public schools across the five boroughs, and stakeholders also have an opportunity to submit written electronic testimony by Jan. 31, 2024 at 5 p.m.

Some advocates have formed an unofficial working group to help prepare stakeholders for oral testimony. Paullette Healy, a parent of two public school students with Individualized Education Programs and a special education and District 75 community advocate, is one of the advocates against mayoral control. Healy said she first learned of mayoral control after having trouble finding a school for her son.

“It wasn’t until I was trying to find out where my son was going to go for middle school in the district that I live in, in District 20, and I was told that there was no middle school setting for my child or children like him,” Healy said. “But, in my journey, I learned how detrimental mayoral control has been to our children and to our families across New York City.”

A former New York City public school student during the time before mayoral control, Healy noted the changes in the school system as a current public school parent, which she said are “directly related to mayoral control.”

In 2002, newly elected Mayor Michael Bloomberg was granted control of the city’s schools. Bloomberg had the power to appoint the city’s Department of Education chancellor and a majority of the Panel for Education Policy, which serves as the Department of Education’s oversight body and approves fiscal and administrative policy decisions that govern the city’s public schools.

However, the mayor does not have an unlimited time frame for control, as the state’s legislature must renew mayoral control every one to two years.

Olivia Swisher, a middle school art teacher and member of the unofficial working group, said the differences between mayoral control and the school board system utilized by many other school districts nationwide exemplifies a “stark contrast of where power lies in our school system.” Swisher, who previously taught in Georgia, is also a member of the Movement of Rank and File Educators, a caucus of the United Federation of Teachers.

“I’ve been under two systems of school governance,” Swisher said. “I’ve been under a locally elected school board and then I moved here and I’m working under mayoral control. And just in terms of how decisions are made, and who has the power to make those decisions, it’s a stark contrast of where power lies in our school system, and what ability teachers, parents, students have to influence their own education.”

When the state extended mayoral control in 2022, the legislature made several changes to the Panel for Education Policy.

While the state kept the borough president’s appointees to five members of each borough, the big changes came with the expansion of the panel from 15 to 23 total members. The state also increased the number of mayoral appointees to the panel, from nine to 13 members, four of whom have to be public school parents, including a parent of a child with an Individualized Education Program, a parent of a child in a bilingual or English as a second language program, and a parent of a child attending a District 75 school or program.

The other changes to the panel include four more seats from the Community Education Councils.

Naveed Hasan, SEAS ’00, ’02, and current panel member, said he experienced a “culture shock” when he joined the panel earlier this year. Hasan previously served as the treasurer and chair of the multilingual committee of Community Education Council 3.

“I feel like it’s one of the most immediate sort of culture shocks and the friction that you have between whatever the mayor may or may not want, not really aligning with individual neighborhoods’ needs from their schools,” Hasan said. “Almost every public school issue is the result of the governance structure of mayoral control not really working. It doesn’t work for anybody, except for the mayor.”

Hasan ran for the new position as the Manhattan Community Education Council representative on the panel that was created by New York state. The 23 Panel for Education Policy members, including Hasan, the chancellor, and the city’s comptroller complete the panel, acting as ex officio members. The panel also includes two high school students who serve as nonvoting members.

The community education council organizes monthly meetings for parents and community members to express concerns, ask questions, and raise issues for the council. They also provide recommendations to the Panel for Education Policy.

Tajh Sutton, community education council president for District 14, said a discrepancy exists between the role of the councils in the education system and their authority.

“The idea and the framing we were always given in the parents’ space is that the community education councils, and again sort of too the school boards, were corrupt,” Sutton said. “The school boards were corrupt and were replaced by this great thing that supports parent engagement. But the CEC is very much similar to the PEP—just very performative spaces where parents are tokenized rather than actually allowed to have a voice and a vote and any real structural or systemic power within the Department of Education.”

Sutton said that without her experience and involvement in the community education council, she wouldn’t have been aware of mayoral control. For Sutton, part of the work she and other advocates have to do is educate parents about mayoral control.

“Your average New York City taxpayer does not even realize that we are under this system and has no idea how it is impacting them,” Sutton said. “They understand they don’t want public schools defunded, libraries defunded, immigrant children attacked and experiencing xenophobia. But because they aren’t even aware of this system, they can’t connect those dots.”

Jia Lee is a special education teacher and member of the unofficial working group. Lee started teaching in 2001, the year before Mayor Bloomberg was given control over public schools. Competitiveness within the public school system was one of the main reasons Lee got involved in advocating for the end of mayoral control.

“The biggest thing, impact for me is feeling like we were in a system based on competition with each other,” Lee said. “Schools need to be places where we’re not in competition with each other. Actually, we should be making all of the schools places they want to send our kids.”

Lee said some changes to the system, like the Fair Student Funding Formula, were “corporate” and fostered rivalry among schools.

“Because of the funding formula, schools compete for students, and in order to do that they appeal to things like really erroneous like standardized test scores or very superficial things, and that pushes schools to be very narrowly focused,” Lee said. “But it focuses so much on these narrow metrics. And then it strips away everything else that makes learning just like meaningful and student-centered and culturally responsive.”

Similarly, Alexandra Haridopolos, a former New York City public school student and current high school teacher, said she is also working to inform parents and other stakeholders about mayoral control. Haridopolos, like other advocates, does not have high expectations of the hearings. However, some, like Hasan, are hopeful that mayoral control will end.

“I’m hopeful because we’re lucky that we have Mayor Adams to run against on this issue because there are so many other problems in terms of being sort of an upright and competent mayor and executing in different parts of the administration,” Hasan said. “Many things are falling apart all at the same time. It’s hard for us right now, but in terms of advocating for a change in the way things are run, it’s helpful.

Hasan said that he would like to see a return to a locally elected school board system that many districts nationwide use. He said this would ensure “equitable voice and representation.”

“The model of everywhere, like 99-point-something percent of the country, uses local school boards and those school board members are elected by the community and they’re held accountable for the outcome of their schools and the children’s education and welfare,” Hasan said.
